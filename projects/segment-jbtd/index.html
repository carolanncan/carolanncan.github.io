Defining the Customer

What people think they want and what they actually want are often not the same thing. To make something people want, it’s important to know what people think they want.

People do not want a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter inch hole.”

Theodore Levitt

People buy products and services to get jobs done. While products come and go, the underlying job-to-be-done does not go away. This notion is at the heart of jobs-to-be-done theory. The purpose of thinking about “jobs” is to force yourself to think about your customer’s current situation and what they are motivated to do, rather than think through the lens of existing products and solutions. The research process

A fellow designer and I conducted one-on-one interviews with 60 customers, a mix of self-service and business tiers. We hoped to learn the first thought that lead customers to decide our product was a something useful to them.

We wanted to understand the context of our customers and the challenges they face in finding and using our product through the framework of forces:

  • The situations that pushed them to needing/wanting something like Segment.

  • What drew customers to Segment.

  • Habits that make it difficult to change to a new product or way of doing things.

  • Anxieties that cause them to have a bad experience or hesitations with the product.

Understanding each of these quadrants for our customers helps us know exactly the job they are trying to do and how we might create solutions for them that they actually want.

How different jobs relate to each other

Jobs can have huge variety in granularity. Many smaller jobs collectively do a bigger job. For example, if you have a job of decorating your house, there are smaller jobs-to-be-done of hanging pictures, buying furniture, knocking down walls, etc.

You can think of jobs as being nested, in a tree-like structure. Imagine each node representing a “job”. This is just an easy way to illustrate hierarchy and parent-child relationships. This keeps product planning focused on what the product is and is not, what should be built and what should not.

If we use a tree to diagram the job of “chat” for Slack, you can see that Facebook, and Dropbox have a similar child job of chat or conversation, but if you follow that child to its parent, the purpose of each of those products are all very different.

The role of the “parent” job

The parent job (or node) is the larger job that our product is fulfilling; the solution that people *actually *want. All child jobs are derived from the parent. Child jobs may seem very similar or almost the same across different parents, but if you understand the parent-child relationship, you can easily know how to stay focused on the parent job you ultimately want to succeed with.

Child jobs are where smaller, more specific jobs like “send data to analytics tools” or “reduce Marketing team dependence on developer resources” are represented. These are the solutions people *think* they want. Any siblings within our parent job is a job for which Segment may want to build a solution.

The role of the “gateway” job

Each of the “child” jobs have the potential to be what we call “gateways”. These are often the product people think they want.

Since gateway jobs meet a more narrowly scoped and specific need, they are easier to identify with. It is the more tangible version of the problem to solve. Because of this, the gateway job can be critical to a potential customer or existing customer understanding that a product meets their need—it’s what initially draws them to Segment.

Segment’s parent job

Not surprisingly, every customer ultimately begins their journey toward Segment with the push of needing or wanting to make better decisions based on data.

What I've called the “Segment job” is the product we believe people actually want. Using the job-to-be-done statement syntax, the Segment job can be expressed as:

When I want to set myself up to easily make decisions from data, I want to eliminate the friction of collecting my data, so I can use it in all the places I need it.”

Segment’s current gateway job

If the parent job is the product people actually want, the gateway job is the product people think they. Again, using the job-to-be-done statement syntax, the Gateway job can be expressed as:

When I implement an analytics tool, and I realize there are barriers to implementing these tools, I want to make the implementation as simple as possible, so I can send data to analytics tools with ease.”

The gateway job is where many (not all) of our customers come to know about Segment. It is their original intent and the job that needs to deliver them an excellent experience. Customers who have found Segment to successfully solve the problem of Gateway job typically go on to find value in Segment above and beyond that.

The product has an opportunity to surprise and delight our customers when they can find more value in the product than they came for. These customers will find new ways to use the product within their teams to scale their usage of Segment with the needs of their organizations. When these customers are hired to new jobs they take Segment with them.

Customer Cohorts

Based on the parent job and the gateway job, I grouped our customers in to three cohorts:

1 - Stays at Gateway job

These customers come in to Segment through the gateway job but don’t seem to expand their use of Segment beyond connecting analytics tools. Their concern is mainly with ease of implementation.

2 - Gateway job to Parent job

A larger amount of customers start at the gateway job, discover other jobs they can accomplish with the product, and come to think about us differently than when they initially came in. They come to realize the product as the product they actually wanted, and not the product they thought they wanted.

3 - Straight to Parent job

Customers more familiar with data and analytics tend to view our product through the lens of the Segment job, rather than *only *the gateway job. They’re thinking about the future of their data infrastructure and want to make sure the choices they make now will scale with them in the future.

Jobs to guide decision making

With our jobs defined, the team has a better idea of the value propositions that appeal to our customers and when those value propositions become relevant to them. It's now easier to orchestrate efforts across our whole team to strategically grow the customer base and help those customers get the most value out of Segment.
