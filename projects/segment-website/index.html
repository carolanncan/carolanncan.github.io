Redesigning the Segment website

The challenge for this project was to make Segment easier to understand through the marketing site. We redesigned the site to give visitors a more clear idea of what the product does and how it might help them.

Problems to solve for:

  • There wasn't a clear strategy for how we were marketing the product to the outside world and who the target audience should be.

  • The Customer Success team and the Sales team were spending time answering questions in support tickets and product demos that should've been easily answered by our marketing site.

  • Communicating the value of Segment was getting derailed by technical jargon and a bit of graphic clutter.

Understanding the problem

Referencing the Jobs to Be Done research I performed a few months ahead of this project, I looked at the job our customers are hiring Segment to help them with.

The Jobs-to-be-done method helped me frame the messaging for the page. It was helpful to focus the message on exactly what a visitor to the site would want to know about the product:

  • Why someone would care about the product

  • What job they hire the product to do

  • How the product works to help you do that job

Background research:

In Nancy Duarte's Tedx Talk she describes her research into some of history's most compelling patterns of communication. This idea inspired us to structure our story around the customer's life before Segment compared to the potential of the customer's life after Segment.

This idea influenced how we wrote the copy for the site and the illustrations we created. You can see an example of that method used in the graphic below, which we used on the final version of the site.

Diverging

I made use of brainmapping, rapid brainstorming, and sketching exercises to explore a wide range of possibilities for writing and illustrating the story of the job to be done.

Converging

With feedback from my team, I narrowed down the options that were the most compelling and fit with our brand. With the general direction of the story defined, I repeated the process of iteration with copy and illustrations.

Testing and prototyping

I set up interviews with a few acquaintances and friends that fit into our target audience but aren't too familiar with Segment.

In the interviews, I asked them to say their thoughts out loud while they were looking at the home page prototype. This helped me gauge the effectiveness of the copy, graphics, and overall structure of the message.

The end result

With the feedback we received from testing our prototypes, I built a mockup of the home page and built the final version in HTML/CSS.

The Success team has reported an overall decrease in support tickets asking general questions about the product.

The Sales team has helped contribute to and rally behind the value propositions we use to familiarize new customers with the product.

See the full version live at Segment.com

Takeaways

A visitor doesn't need to understand everything about the product.

They just need enough to understand and remember what your product is and how the could benefit from it.

Keep the copy short and sweet.

Visitors aren't likely to spend long reading the text on your site.

Talk like your customers.

In your marketing copy, use vocabulary that your customers use. They'll easily understand and relate to your product.

Test the comprehension of your graphics!

Most of our early graphics were clever by our standards but confusing to people outside of the company.

Contrast the old way with the new way.

Contrasting the old way of doing things with the new way of doing things is a very quick and compelling way to communicate the value of something.
