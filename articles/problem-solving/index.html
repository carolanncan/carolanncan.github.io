A problem-solving model

The inspiration for this outline came from working with the Google Ventures Design Sprint model and the Design Council's Double Diamond model. I wanted to take the opportunity to make an outline of each step of the problem-solving process from what I found valuable about both of those models as neither have been a perfect fit for my experiences.

The process by which we define problems and the way in which we solve them has a significant impact on the form the solution takes. This is only an outline of the general process I use when solving design problems for a business. It doesn't describe the inherent messiness of solving a problem.

Understand

To correctly define a problem, you need a complete picture of what is creating the problem. This is why it's important to understand the circumstances and forces surrounding a situation before trying to define a problem and arrive at a solution.

Goals

  • Identify the project goals (desired outcomes).

  • Establish common understanding and context for why it is useful to take on a new project.

  • Get to the root of a problem.

Information to gather

  • The initial idea, inspiration, or issue that initiated this project.

  • How the business is currently being impacted in this area?

  • How the business will be impacted in the future if this area isn't addressed?

  • How we are solving the problem right now?

    • Is there a lack of a solution in place? Is there a straw-man solution in place?

    • What's working well about how we solve the problem right now? What's not working well?

  • How other businesses are solving similar problems?

  • The desired out come of the project and what success looks like.

  • The areas of the company that will receive the most immediate benefit from this project.

Logistics

  • What are the goals of this project?

  • Who are all of the stakeholders in this project?

  • Who is the owner of this project?

    • The owner is responsible for making sure the project is going in the right direction, and helping the team stay in-step.

Define

Every decision made from this point on will be working to resolve the problem statement. So it's important to make sure the problem statement continues to reflect the root problem to be solved. Otherwise, we might define the problem too narrowly or design solutions for adjacent problems without ever fixing the causal problem.

Throughout the rest of the process, more will be learned which will shed new light on underlying problems. Revisit this stage and iterate on the problem statement if the project needs re-calibration.

Goals

  • Identify the project objectives.

  • Write a clearly defined problem statement.

  • Define the scope of the problem.

How do we reach our goals?

  1. Analyze the information from the Understand phase.

  2. Synthesize the information to create a list of the issues that need to be addressed.

  3. Create a single problem statement that defines the objective for this project.

Explore

The purpose of this stage is to fully explore viable solutions for the problem. It's tempting to want to implement the first solution that comes to mind. But creative exploration will be valuable here to find the best way to solve the problem. The purpose of this stage is to narrow down the possible solutions, understand and test their strengths and weaknesses, and then choose the best solution to implement in the final Build stage.

Goals

  • Identify the strategy we will use to achieve our objectives.

  • Build a prototype that reflects what will be implemented.

What do we do in this step?

  • Exhaust all the potential solutions for what we build

  • Refine ideas by quickly iterating and testing them out

  • Narrow down the best ideas

  • Build a final prototype to take in to development

How do we do that?

  • Brainstorming exercises

  • Critiques

  • Prototyping and user testing

Build

Similar to the scientific method, we have a problem statement and a hypothesis to test if our understanding and solution is correct. The Build stage is where we manifest our hypothesis in order to test it and learn from it. We need to know if the solution is successful and how we might improve it. The two critical parts of this stage are: (1) to bring the solution to life through implementation and (2) to create a system for measuring the success of the solution.

Goals

  • Identify the tactics

  • Implement the solution

  • Create a system to measure success

  • Systematize maintenance and iteration

  • Share what has been learned

What are we doing in this step?

  • Define the MVP

    • This is what is considered to be essential to achieve success in the objectives defined in the Discover phase.

  • Identify the "nice-to-have's"

    • These are things that are not critical to the success of the project but have value and purpose at this stage. They usually need to be left out due to time constraints or technical limitations.

  • Begin building.

    • What you will build in this stage is based off of the prototype developed in the Explore stage.

  • Continue to test and iterate to reach a polished version that is ready to launch.

  • Make sure tracking is in place so we can learn about usage.

  • Establish the process for getting continual user feedback post-launch and how that feedback will be incorporated to quickly improve the product.

How do we do that?

  • Brainstorming exercises

  • Critiques

  • Prototyping and user testing

  • User interviews and research

In conclusion

In a way, I hate outlines like these. They make problem-solving seem deceptively easy or can make you feel like you're failing at the process if things are a bit messy.

What you don't see in an outline is the moving back and forth between stages, the many moments of self-doubt, the assumptions and risks you sometimes have to make to move forward, and all the times you end up being wrong.

Hopefully what you do see here is an outline that makes problem-solving accessible. While it's not always easy, it is something that anyone can excel at if the time is taken to appreciate the process.
