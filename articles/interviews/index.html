Tips and Tricks

Optimizing your user interviews

I used to be intimidated by the idea of participating in, leading, and coordinating user interviews. I was sure that there was a very correct process for the whole thing that I'd mess up. Now that I've had more experience with interviews and the research process, I wanted to share some of the things I've learned from trial and error.

It's not nearly as scary or unforgiving as I had imagined. You can be precise without trying to be too careful. And the more you do it the easier it gets. Hopefully a few of these tips help you get started leading your own interviews.

Interviewing Methods

Quantitative vs. qualitative

Quantitative is good for understanding what, where, and when. Qualitative is good for understanding why and how.

In-person vs. survey

Surveys are great for finding a consensus. In-person interviews are great for understanding someone’s thought process, emotions, and environment.

Which method to use?

If you’re just starting out with your research, user-interviews can be great. It can act as a blunt tool. Surveys are more like a surgical knife. They're a great opportunity to ask more specific questions that produce answers smaller in scope.

Sourcing interviewees

Intercom is excellent for finding segments of existing customers based on their behavior with your product or website.

If you need to interview people who aren't familiar with your product, personal acquaintances have been the most fruitful for me. I've had mixed experiences with services like Usertesting.com, validately.com, etc. I'm still experimenting, but in the past, the testers haven't been a great fit for what we were trying to learn.

Scheduling

Calendly

Calendly is a lifesaver for booking times on the calendar. It takes all of the work out of coordinating availability, time zone differences, and setting up the calendar invites.

Using a service like Calendly makes it easier on the interviewee to find a time most convenient for them and you don’t get caught trying to juggle a myriad of potential times you suggested to different people.

When not to schedule an interview

After learning this the hard way, I now always leave 30min breaks between bookings (another feature of Calendly). This time is valuable for catching up on last minute notes you need to jot down or debrief with someone on what you learned from the interview.

And if you're like me, you'll be ready for a few minutes to chill. Introversion is real.

Take my word for it, don't schedule interviews on Fridays. You probably won’t be in the mood after a long week of interviews and neither will your interviewee. Work days can be very lax/fluid on Fridays. Having a time constraint is just kind of a bummer.

How to be prepared

Know how many people you need to interview and be sure to schedule a few more interviews than you need. People are busy and schedules can be chaotic; some people will inevitably have to cancel last minute.

Send reminder emails the morning of the interview. While an extra email might be annoying, it decreases the risk that they’ll forget about the interview or confuse the start time. People have busy lives it happens!

Record who you’ve reached out to (Intercom has a nice "last contacted" filter). I'm a fan of Trello. I keep a trello board of the interview roster which helps me easily keep track of what's up next and what has been completed.

Interview in pairs if possible. One person acts as the conversation lead which allows them to focus on what the interviewee is saying and where to dig in further with more specific questions.

The second person acts as the note taker. They are focused on recording what the interviewee is saying. This means they can take the time to record more than just the words being said but also the emotions expressed, how long it took the interviewee to recall certain information, etc.

Background research

Research some biographical information about the person you're about to speak with. I ususally start by collecting the basics: photo, name, what they do, where the live, etc. Twitter is always good for that. With that info, you're armed with some info to be casual and friendly if you need something to get the convo started!

It's also important to collect their most recent usage of your product. Salesforce with Clearbit integrated is a quick stop for this. You can get an overview of any conversations/emails they’ve exchanged with your sales tea and support team.

Good conversation

Like having a beer

A lot of the interview will come down to just having some good conversation. Don't worry about trying to get all the answers you need. Just be casual and be yourself. The interview should be fun! I always imagine the tone of the conversation being similar to grabbing coffee or beer with a friend.

Leave the awkward silence

Your job as the interviewer is to do more listening than talking. If you try to fill in the silence you might influence the interviewee's answer and disrupt their thinking. And for the interviewee, the silence really isn’t that awkward.

Be sure not suggest answers to fill in the space or out of nervousness. Expand on a question or give examples if you are asked to. You might sway their answer.

Karma

Always take the last minute or two to ask how you can return the favor. Follow through on anything your promise to do in return! And of course, only promise things you know that you can follow through on.
